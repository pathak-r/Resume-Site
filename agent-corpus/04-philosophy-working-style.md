## Product philosophy

- The prototype-first approach — one story where it paid off, one where it cost you:

we mocked up the end to end user flow for the new redesigned smart3d UI / UX ran it with various stakeholders and customers before building. saved us from building some really wrong stuff that couldve set us back by 6 months to a year.

- How you decide what to build (frameworks you actually use vs. judgment):

depending on bandwidth (which is not unlimited oviously) - importance and impact to customer and then ranked by dev effort roughly speaking but then case by case judgement too. 

- How you think about AI products specifically (what's different about PM'ing LLM
  products vs. classic SaaS — evals, non-determinism, demos-vs-products):

i'd say evals and non determinism definitely where applicable. being able to quickly get where you want to definitely does save a lot of cycle time. we are bolder , more willing to take bold steps and ideas. 

## Working with people

- With engineers (your credibility move, your failure mode):

to work with engineers always gain subject matter expertise of the product, as much as possible. while communicating always communicate in data. as in i am not saying this, the data is saying this. or the data says ... . if you are prepared that way you can execute effectively and also gain respect. that way they want to listen to you. 

- With sales / customers (enterprise context — how you handle "the big account wants X"):

big account wants X but other big accounts dont want X and therefore you need to put into context the request and whether it is worth building, if the data says no, you gotta say no but in a way that doesnt sound like a no. "we currently dont have the bandwidth but will keep you posted" or we could look at building SOME of what the big account is requesting if not the whole thing if that mnakes sense.

- A real disagreement with leadership — what happened, how it resolved:

tech transition of smart 3d should have been priority 1 , multi threading and VB6 to .NET + WPF. that alone wouldve given a massive benefit , esp multi threading since the produc t wouldve become much faster. the UI/UX redesign couldve waited. there was no need to do it in parallel. this was my opinion and where i disagreed with leadership. did not get resolved , i still let them know and at some level i believe they saw my point. 

## Self-assessment

- A genuine failure and what changed because of it:

we tried to implement the progressive disclosure UI/ UX philosophy to redesign the UI / UX. turns out (and fact is) - the philosophy is not suited for a product like smart3d. the real failure was that we were actually baking it right in. this was happening just as i was taking over as PM. some time was wasted. result was, and i insstituted this (which is common sense best practice actually) - process changed to where UI / UX mockups must be made before writing a single line of code, circulated, pressure tested and approved before we move forward. 

- Your honest weakness (not a humblebrag — the agent will be asked and evasion reads
  worse than honesty):

i can tend to be impatient. if i feel like someones getting in the way there have been times when things have flared up a bit. I've learnt to be better though. 

- What kind of manager / environment gets your best work:

manager and environment where subject matter expertise is continuously pursued. actions are goal oriented and not process oriented (within reasonable and sensible boundaries of course).

- How you stay current on AI (specific sources, habits, the personal-build pipeline):

keep building stuff, of different kinds. nothing teaches better than doing it yourself.
